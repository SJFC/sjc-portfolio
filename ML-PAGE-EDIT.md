### Argument I

**Heading:** The portrait as data visualisation
**Change to:**

**Body text:**
A Victorian portrait was never a neutral record. It encoded social position, gender, propriety, wealth — the values of whoever commissioned it. It is a closed dataset: it shows only what the system valued enough to keep. The collages do not refuse the portrait. They work inside it, then dissolve it from within — the face replaced not by absence, but by light.

### Argument II

**Heading:** Time as non-linear, space as relational
**Change to:**

**Body text:**
The lantern slide fixed time: arrest a moment, hold it under glass, project it, call it true. This work refuses that. Lefebvre's *half-glimpsed forgotten spaces* — the wayside shrine, the overgrown spring, the local deity not quite erased — are the residual spaces the official image cannot fully contain. Massey's relational space sits alongside: images from different times and registers, layered, because space and time are constellations of relation rather than fixed coordinates.

Imagine you're a labyrinth, your veins
stone passages draped velvet-wet with moss.
You hear the ring of footsteps, then these stop
and voices whisper as they find a key
that turns into a snatch of song, a breeze
of fade and shimmer squeezing to become
a wing-like thing that scrapes at hardwood
doors. An amber warmth – a breath – a flare
unlocks a box where secrets carved from games
of chance are trapped in spotted maps of mirror glass.
And then, uncertain candle-shadows drip
their molten wax on golden threads that lead
to yet another wall. Dear Visitor,
what brought you to these lairs of fractal spaces
built up over years, secreting spiral
trails which split and merge like pools of liquid
mercury. I offer – what? An arch
of palimpsests, a history, a vague
attenuated frayed half-world where past
imperfect paper monsters fold themselves
into the safety of this – passé simple – abyss.
